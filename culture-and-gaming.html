Cultural Difference in Gaming Preferences

Previous Next

Motivation

Many researchers have found that gaming experiences and perceptions vary across cultures when the same game is played. As such, game designers are paying special attention when designing a game for a different culture or in a cross-cultural context. Many researchers and practitioners have also explored game design methods that facilitate consideration of cultural differences. However, methods used in previous research were based on subjective analysis of cultural differences and non-empirical mapping between cultural conflicts and game design components. In other words, there is little knowledge as to the relationships among cultural value differences and game play preferences beyond deductive speculations.

Research Outcomes

I conducted interviews with seven gamers from five countries to understand their gaming preferences. After each interview, I used Hofstede’s Value Survey Module to evaluate their cultural values.

Hofstede’s model of cultural value distinguishes key characteristics of national cultures, including four major dimensions:

  • Power distance (PDI), which measures the extent to which the less powerful members expect and accept that power is distributed unequally
  • Level of individualism (IDV), which measures the degree to which individuals are independent from each other (as opposed to collectivism, which depicts the degree to which individuals are integrated to groups)
  • Level of masculinity (MAS), which measures the distinction of emotional roles between genders (as opposed to femininity, which depicts the overlap of emotional gender roles)
  • Uncertainty avoidance (UAI), which measures the extent to which the members of a culture feel uncomfortable with ambiguity or unknown

Through inductive content coding, I identified several themes in participants’ gaming preferences that demonstrated a clear cue of influence from their cultural values:

  • Social interaction was associated with player’s value of uncertainty avoidance, individualism/collectivism and power distance
  • Tension and humor in game was associated with high uncertainty acceptance
  • Openness and freedom was related to individualism.
