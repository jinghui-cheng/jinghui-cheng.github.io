Support for Deaf Students in a Hearing School

Motivation

Deaf and hard of hearing (DHH) students preferred attending mainstream schools because of the academic advantages. However, there are many problems for them to access educational resources in mainstream schools, even though the use of Assistive Technology (AT) is mandated by law. Little knowledge is established as to the current status of AT use for DHH students in a mainstream school.

Research Outcomes

In an exploratory case study, we conducted email conversations and a 30-minute face-to-face interview with a Disability Accommodation Coordinator in a hearing law school in the Chicago area, where there is one deaf student who received cochlear implant. We found that:

  • The school only provided one type of AT (an open captioning system that relies on a transcriber) to the deaf student.
  • The procedure and logistics of acquiring the AT for the deaf student is not well supported.
  • The student didn’t face great difficulties in general interaction with hearing people, but encountered problems interacting in highly dynamic environments (e.g. mock trial and trial education courses)

Based on these insights, we suggest that (1) future research on AT for DHH students should emphasize more on facilitating real-time communication in highly dynamic interactions, (2) software systems could be developed to take care of the logistics of acquiring AT, and (3) more researches could be conducted to investigate the exploitation of new technologies for deaf students.
